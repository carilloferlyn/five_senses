The five sensory 'gatekeepers' of our bodies taste, smell, touch, sight and hearing help us survive. Every day, our ears, nose, tongue, eyes and skin are constantly bombarded with massive amounts of information from the environment around us that must be processed and responded to. You use them every day to get to know and understand the world around you. What are they? Your senses! The five senses — seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, and touching — help us to learn about our surroundings. The following lessons will help your students explore their senses.


Sense of Sight Watch Me!
Sense of Sightclose

The sense of sight is the ability of the eyes to see objects around you. It is also the ability to perceive colour, brightness, depth. The sense of sight is considered to be one of the most complex of the five human senses. The eyeball is the organ that allows vision.

Sense of Smell Watch Me!
Sense of Smellclose

The sense of smell, or olfaction, is the special sense through which smells (or odors) are perceived. The sense of smell has many functions, including detecting desirable foods, hazards, and pheromones, and plays a role in taste.

Sense of Touch Watch Me!
Sense of Touchclose

The ability to perceive an object or other stimulus that comes into contact with the surface of the skin (e.g., by pressure, stroking). Also called tactile sense. See haptic perception; tactile perception. See also cutaneous sense.






Sense of Hearing Watch Me!
Sense of Hearingclose

The ability to hear; the auditory faculty. synonyms: audition, auditory modality, auditory sense, hearing. types: ear.

Sense of Tastemore_vert Watch Me!
Sense of Tasteclose

The faculty of distinguishing sweet, sour, bitter, and salty properties in the mouth. “his cold deprived him of his sense of taste” synonyms: gustation, gustatory modality, taste.


Story Time!!!

Hellen Keller (1880-1968) lived in world of darkness and silence. She was blind and deaf. She could neither see nor hear. Yet, she became one of the America's most famous author and lecturer. She had a very good teacher Anne Sullivan. Anne made Helen feel the objects with one hand. Then she traced the word for each object onto the palm of Helen's hand.
